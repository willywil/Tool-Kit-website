Static Gasses

Gasses are in a static state when they are not in motion -like the air in a balloon.

Specific Gas Constant (Rspecific)

Simply Put: In metric units, the specific gas constant of any gas tells you how much enthalpy would be present if you had a kilogram of that gas at one degree kelvin. In U.S. units, the specific gas constant of any gas tells you how much enthalpy would be present if you had a slug of that gas at one degree Ranikie. It's just the ratio of the gas constant (8.314 J/(mol•K) in metric or ft-lb/(slug•R) in U.S.) to the molecular weight of the gas.

Charles' Law

Simply Put: Heating a gas will cause it to expand. Cooling a gas will cause it to compress. It is a proportional relationship. They always increase or decrease together, as long as the pressure is constant.
T ∝ V


Gay-Lussac's Law

Simply Put: If the gas cannot expand, then heating the gas will cause it to build pressure. If the gas cannot expand, then cooling the gas will lower its pressure. It is a proportional relationship. They always increase or decrease together, as long as the volume is constant.

T ∝ p

Boyles' Law

Simply Put: If the temperature does not change at all, compressing a gas will cause it to build pressure. If the temperature does not change at all, allowing a gas to expand will lower its pressure. It is a proportional relationship. They always increase or decrease together, as long as the temperature is constant.
p ∝ V

Equation of State

Simply Put: Static pressure is the product of density, static temperature, and the specific gas constant of the gas. The equation of state is a combination of Boyles' Law, Gay-Lussac Law, and Charles' Law. It sums up all the other laws about static gasses. As long as you know two of the three variables (T, p, or rho)

p=ρ•Rspecific•T

Air at High Altitudes

Aerospace engineering is all about designing objects that will achieve high altitudes. No discussion on aerospace engineering could be complete without the atmosphere at high altitudes. Because the weather is constantly changing, aerospace engineers use a table of values that represent the average atmospheric conditions at various altitudes assuming perfect weather. It's called The Standard Atmosphere.

Reading the data from The Standard Atmosphere

Simply Put: Many times, pressure, temperature, and density are given as the altitude in which those values could be found. Pressure can be given as the altitiude at which the air pressure would be at that value. It's called the 'pressure-altitude'. Temerature can be given as the altitiude at which the air pressure would be at that value. It's called the 'temperature-altitude'. Density can be given as the altitiude at which the air pressure would be at that value. It's called the 'density-altitude'. The Standard Atmosphere only gives values every 500ft or 100km. So if you have a pressure, temperature, or density value that falls between two points, you must use linear intropolation to find the correct altitude value for the measurement. This is the equation for linear intropolation:

Air Temperature at Altitude

Simply Put: Air temperature will decrease as the altitude increases into the troposphere. It is a linear relationship. Between the troposphere and the stratusphere, the air temperature will be constant for several kilometers (or feet in U.S.). This is called the tropopause. As the altitude increases into the stratusphere, the temperature will begin to increase. It will almost return to the temperature at sea level. It is a linear relationship. Between the troposphere and the stratusphere, the air temperature will be constant for several kilometers (or feet in U.S.). This is called the stratopause. Air temperature will begin to decrease again as the altitude increases into the mesosphere. It is a linear relationship. Between the mesosphere and the thermosphere, the air temperature will be constant for several kilometers (or feet in U.S.). This is called the mesopause. At altitudes in the Termosphere, the temperature will rise to very very high levels, much higher than the temperature at sea level. Aerodynamics

An object moving through the air will have the same result as the same air moving over an object as long as the similarity parameters are the same. This allows engineers to create scale models and test them in wind tunnels. There are two similarity parameters that must be the same for every wind tunnel simulation.

Mach Number (Similarity Parameter 1)

Simply Put: The mach number tells you how fast an object is moving relative to the speed of sound in that gas. A mach number of .3 means that the object is traveling at 30% of speed of sound. If the mach number is lass than .3, the flow is considered 'incompressible'. If the mach number is greater than .3, the flow is considered 'compressible'. If the Mach number is less than 1, the flow is subsonic. If the Mach number is greater than 1 the flow is supersonic. If the Mach number is greater than 3, the flow is considered supersonic. The Effects of Speed

Simply Put: The faster a fluid is traveling, the lower it's static pressure will become. Becuase pressure is proportional to temperature (Gay-Lussac Law), the fluid cool as well. At any speed higher than Mach .3, the fluid will begin to expand. This is called "compressible flow". Despite the name, compressible flow does not actually cause the fluid to compress at all. Instead the fluid will expand, leading to a drop in fluid density.
